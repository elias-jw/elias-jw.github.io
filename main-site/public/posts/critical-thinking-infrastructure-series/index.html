Critical thinking infrastructure: an essay series

April 9, 2026

This is a series of essays on building infrastructure for better decision-making. The end-goal is to allow decision makers dealing with high-pressure and time-sensitive problems, to ask and get defensible answers in minutes, instead of weeks and months.

The immediate context is logistics and supply chain planning, though the ideas reach further. The argument is that the bottleneck in most high-stakes decisions is not the absence of data or models but the absence of infrastructure for asking good questions quickly. The essays cover shortcomings in existing decision models, the technical architecture of a solution, AI, and the human layer.

This series accompanies my application to the Encode AI fellowship.

1. When deliberation meets reality (Mar 2026)

The starting point. Examines why existing decision-making frameworks (deliberative democracy platforms, multi-criteria analysis, problem structuring methods) consistently fail under real-world constraints of time, politics, and incomplete information. Argues that the missing piece is not better deliberation but faster iteration.

2. Building critical thinking infrastructure (Apr 2026)

The architecture. Introduces nine design principles for a platform that compresses the explore-decide loop: ask a question, get an answer, ask a better one. Covers how to separate stable spatial knowledge from volatile project data, why data objects must carry no behaviour, and how materialisation should be an economic decision rather than a structural one.

3. No single model will save us (Apr 2026)

The technical argument for hybrid architecture. Explains why neither a general-purpose LLM nor a specialised solver alone is sufficient for logistics reasoning, and how a layered architecture (spatial knowledge graph, domain ontology, compute adapters, and AI facilitator) outperforms any single model on the tasks that actually matter.

4. The Social Layer: Modelling How Decision-Makers Think About Problems (Apr 2026)

The human extension. Good infrastructure for decisions must model not just the physical problem but the people reasoning about it: their mental models, their priors, and how those shift as evidence arrives. Introduces a social layer that sits above the technical architecture and represents how decision-makers actually frame problems.

5. Hypothesis register: collective flourishing under real-world constraints (Apr 2026)

The bets. A live register of the hypotheses the platform is designed to test, including the core claim that compressing time-to-question and time-to-answer changes the quality of decisions, not just the speed of making them. Structured so each hypothesis is falsifiable and tied to a measurable outcome.

6. Mapping the UK public good case: a Wardley analysis of the architecture (Apr 2026)

The public good framing. A Wardley map of the platform positioned as public infrastructure. The argument: grounded, inspectable decision support for physical operations is an under-provided public good, with the UK supply chain resilience agenda as the immediate context.

7. Mapping the venture case: a Wardley analysis of the architecture (Apr 2026)

The commercial framing. The same architecture mapped from a venture-backed company perspective. Identifies where value is evolving on the stack, why the substrate layer is underfunded relative to where capital will flow next, and what events would force a revision of the map.
